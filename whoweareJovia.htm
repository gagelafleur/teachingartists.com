JOVIA ARMSTRONG

ILLINOIS (Chicago)		  
   

Daringly eclectic and innovative percussionist JoVia Armstrong, a Detroit native, is a well-traveled composer and Teaching Artist.  She developed "Sounds About Write,” a curriculum to teach students musical genre, melody, chords and rhythm through the exploration of electronic instruments and software.  She has performed with El DeBarge, Rahsaan Patterson, Trey Anastacio, Frank McComb, Res, Omar and Michael Franti & Spearhead to name a few.  She is a writer behind world Jazz group Musique Noire that released their debut CD "Good Hair" in 2008.  They were nominated for three Detroit Music Awards and JoVia was nominated for an individual award for Best World Musician.  She released her debut album, "Fuzzy Blue Robe Chronicles" in 2009.   

 

McArthur Foundation
 

What or who inspired you to become a Teaching Artist?

 

I started off needing work to supplement my income.  I’ve always liked teaching so it seemed to be a good idea.  Teaching helps me to learn about my own craft as well.  The more I teach, the better I get at teaching and at my musical skills.

 

What has been most helpful to you in your work?

 

Being vulnerable has been the most helpful.  Being open to learning from my peers and my students helps me to discover various ways to teach.  My goal is to show students the easiest possible way of doing what I do.  I have to be open to their learning curves or obstacles.  We’re all from different cultures and have different interests.  We all learn differently.  I find at least two different ways of teaching everything.

 

What have you learned are the unique talents of a Teaching Artist?

 

The unique talents of Teaching Artists are our ability to connect with students.  Not that other teachers don’t, I just feel like we do most of the time.  Being able to cultivate talents in our students and to give them confidence, no matter what their background is.

 

What have you learned that a Teaching Artist needs to know?

 

A Teachings Artist needs to know the 21st century learning skills, how to be creative teachers, how to understand the communities they are working in, and how adapt to the school environment and culture.

 

What have you learned is the most important thing a Teaching Artists needs to be able to do?

 

Think outside the box by using social media and/or digital technology… (as cliché as it sounds.)   Students will be more engaged if a Teaching Artist delves into the students’ world a bit. This also will help with classroom management.

 

What or who has been your best teacher?

 

My best teacher has been the various challenges, not enough (or no) equipment, not enough prior knowledge, small classroom, broken equipment, unsupportive school staff, etc.  Challenges force a Teaching Artist to create a “dope” curriculum.

 

What is the most important advice you would give to a young artist about to enter the field?

 

Follow your passion and be the best at it… and then release it.  Don’t be a perfectionist.

 

Has there been one experience that immediately comes into your mind that changed the way you teach?

 

The challenges I faced all changed the way I teach. 

 

What in your classroom experience encapsulates your thoughts on the work of a Teaching Artist?

 

I believe that Teaching Artists are the teachers of the future because we aren’t following a boring book of rules, regulations, and someone else’s bland, disengaging curriculum.  We are taking students to a new level of themselves. That to me is banging.

 

What sustained (s) your practice as a Teaching Artist?

 

Hugs.  The kids. For my older students, hearing them tell me that they love my class.  I choose to teach.  I could have moved to NYC or LA a long time ago to just be a musician, but teaching helps me learn more about my craft.
