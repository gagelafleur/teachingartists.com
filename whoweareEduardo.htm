EDUARDO MENDONCA
WASHINGTON (Seattle)		  
   



 		 Eduardo Mendonça received his Masters in Arts Education from the Instituto de Música da Universidade Católica do Salvador (Music Institute of the Catholic University of Salvador) and has been teaching and performing professionally since 1982. Before moving to the United States Mendonça taught in the Salvadoran public schools in the capital of Bahia in Brazil. He is interested in sharing his Brazilian culture and introducing less well-known styles of music to his students in this county. 

 


Arts Corps after school class, Northgate Elementary School

 

What has been most helpful to you in your work?

 

The fact that I mainly dedicate what I do to those with little or no access to the arts. Using the arts as a tool of communication/education fascinates me every single time I do my work.

 

What have you learned are the unique talents of a Teaching Artist?

 

Teaching Artists need to use elements not unique to just the Teaching Artist field in order to be successful. Understanding and practicing Social Justice and facilitating learning while building community are some elements I believe are required to deeply use the arts for social change. However, I believe that using the arts as reinforcement of education and self-expression is a unique talent of a Teaching Artist.

 

What have you learned that a Teaching Artist needs to know?

 

I have learned that Teaching Artists need to know how to plan and how to achieve educational goals, how to investigate and use assessment tools, and to have strong organizational skills needed to merge artistry with academic skills.

 

What have you learned is the most important thing a Teaching Artists needs to be able to do?

              

A Teaching Artist needs to learn the community first, needs to demonstrate genuine interest in learning, needs to respect cultural differences as a starting point to share skills and build on what the community already knows and shares. A Teaching Artist needs to empower students, to listen closely to their ideas, and to share power for an honest relationship.

 

What or who has been your best teacher?

 

I can name three of them in different areas: Neyde Aquino Borges (Arts Education), José Abade de Oliveira (Architecture Design), and Germano Dias Machado (EPB  Studies of Brazilian Politics)

 

What is the most important advice you would give to a young artist about to enter the field?

 

Do it if it is in your heart.

 

Has there been one experience that immediately comes into your mind that changed the way you teach?

 

In 1982 I was hired to teach Arts Education in a public school in Brazil. It was a 5th - 8th grade night program and the legal age to be enrolled was 14 years old. I was younger than most of my students. There was no motivation to learn in the whole school. Students had low performance and terrible academic results. Teachers were tired after a long day teaching in other schools and adding the nighttime job as second job to increase their income. In the same way most of students had hard jobs, low payment, and lived in local shantytowns. The teachers complained and several students slept in the classroom.

 

By listening to both, faculty and students, I completely changed the way I taught. Since I had studied Augusto Boal techniques I applied what I learned from them in the classroom and broke down quarters, applying geometry separately (to support Visual Arts and Math), Visual Arts, Music and Theater. I tried to pair up with the rest of the faculty to integrate arts with their subjects, but my idea was rejected because teachers thought it was just one more thing to add to their stress of teaching in that night program. However, the Portuguese teacher wanted to give a try.

 

I started by changing the way the desks were placed in the classroom. I asked students to tell me the stories in their communities, what happen that day on the way to work or on the way to school. I built my curriculum based in their reality. I used the last two quarters to partner with the Portuguese teacher. The quarter before the last the content was music. I divided each class into small groups and each group worked on an original composition by anyone in the class or they composed from someone they know from their community. For my deadline lyrics were brought into the classroom, and we talked about the content without mentioning grammar or writing styles. The music quarter ended with a music contest for whole school and open to the community.

 

The last quarter was theater. This was the most exciting quarter because the students built the sets applying what they learned in visual arts. The planned the production thinking the way we plan and think logically when we apply geometry. They used their own music and performed plays they wrote using stories they created in the classroom (checked by the Portuguese teacher, of course).

 

As a result, the culture of the school changed. The students demonstrated motivation at the school even after a long day of work. Teachers in the faculty room were talking more positively about the students and their progress. The students were visibly better setting their goals as a group and even started to question the rest of the faculty about changing their teaching methods to help the students to learn.

 

What is your classroom experience encapsulates your thoughts on the work of a Teaching Artist?

 

It is pretty much above. Besides sharing fundamental skills in the art form, with respect, I instigate students to share their cultural diversity, and provoke creative thinking, self-expression and education through arts.

 

What sustained (s) your practice as a Teaching Artist?

 

I see the arts as a bridge that connects people, not as invisible walls that separate civilizations.
