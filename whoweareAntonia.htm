ANTONIA PEREZ
 NEW YORK (New York City)		  
   



 		 Antonia Perez is a visual artist who makes sculpture, paintings and site-specific works from repurposed materials she has collected over time. With a background in painting, and utilizing modernist and minimalist concepts, Perez’s work alludes to art movements like Pattern and Decoration, Pop Art, and Minimalism. She is a recipient of the 2011 Marie Walsh Sharpe Space Program Award. She has exhibited her work locally and nationally, most recently at El Museo del Barrio, Praxis Chelsea, EFA Project Space, and Storefront Bushwick. Perez has worked in the arts education field for over twenty-five years working with students spanning a broad spectrum of cultural and ethnic backgrounds, economic circumstances, ages, abilities and identities. As a Teaching Artist, she has worked with numerous arts and arts education institutions, notably, The Solomon R. Guggenheim’s Learning Through Art Program, The Joan Mitchell Foundation, The Studio in A School Association, Lifetime Arts, Inc., The Brooklyn Museum, and PS 1 Museum. She directed the Education Program at Art in General, a not-for-profit contemporary arts organization, for eight years. She served as a panelist for the New York State Council on the Arts-Arts in Education Panel. She holds a Bachelors Degree in Visual Art and Arts in Education from the State University of New York, Empire State College and a Master of Fine Arts from the City University of New York, Queens College.

 


3rd Grade, PS 88, Queens, through the Learning Through Art program of the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum.
 

 

What or who inspired you to become a Teaching Artist?

 

Becoming a Teaching Artist was not something that I set out to do. It happened organically over time. At first I was inspired by some children, who asked if they could help me paint while I was working on an exterior mural project many years ago in San Diego. I had never taught art, but I gave them each a brush and pointed to the buckets of paint and the wall. I shortly realized that they needed some direction and guidance and showed them how to dip the brush and grasp it in order to paint. It was the first time I had ever considered teaching and it was a learning experience for me as well. During that period I became friends with an early childhood educator, Irma Castro, who had started a free independent community school in my neighborhood. I was very interested in our discussions about education and she encouraged me to start teaching classes in a local art center.

 

What has been most helpful to you in your work?

 

There are so many helpful elements that make my teaching practice successful. These include but are not limited to:

  • Having an active studio practice that includes sufficient time to make my work, opportunities to exhibit, regularly see art on view where I live and participate in residencies.

  • Working in collaboration with committed organizations and cultural and educational institutions the have a genuine appreciation of the value of arts education.

  • Having an ample budget that allows for sufficient materials and time for students to make meaningful artworks.

  • Working with Artist Assistants.

  • Long and short term curriculum and lesson planning.

  • Having access to a wide range of high quality art images to use as reference and inquiry material.

  • Collaborating with classroom teachers and other Teaching Artists.

  • Especially in the early stages of my teaching, many professional development workshops and seminars given by educational specialists and psychologists were helpful in guiding the development of my teaching techniques.

  • Ongoing professional development that expands the breadth and depth of my teaching skills.

  • “Talking shop” with other Teaching Artists.

  • Observing other artists teach.

What have you learned are the unique talents of a Teaching Artist?

 

I have learned that not every artist can teach and not every teacher can teach art. Perhaps some individual talents of Teaching Artists can be found in other teachers and other artists, but I have come to understand that Teaching Artists are a special combination of artist and teacher, whose abilities include great flexibility, imagination, compassion, pedagogical skills, organization, technical skills with a wide range of materials and media, knowledge of art forms, art history and contemporary art, an uncanny ability to draw from that knowledge at just the right moment, and a fearless willingness to take on art education projects of any size. Teaching Artists are creative problem solvers, but they also teach how to solve problems creatively. Teaching Artists use art making to teach about life.

 

What have you learned that a Teaching Artist needs to know?

 

Teaching Artists need to know:

  • Being well prepared is necessary for consistent success of your lessons.

  • Preparation includes: knowing your students’ names and ages, understanding their abilities and interests, knowing something about their background and the community in which they live, knowing what you want them to learn and why that is important, knowing how to approach teaching it, having one or two alternate plans, knowing how much time you have and how you will divide that time in your lesson, having all the materials you need to teach the lesson, preparing materials and/or space before the class is to begin.

  • Your sense of humor is one of your most important assets.

  • How to plan a lesson.

  • How to design a curriculum.

  • How to order sufficient and appropriate materials for the lesson and the age group in the class.

  • How to prepare materials for the lesson.

  • Things don’t always go according to plan.

  • Our own interest and excitement about art is contagious.

  • How to present a lesson to a group (according to age and abilities).

  • How to facilitate individual progress by the students in the class.

  • How to help struggling students.

  • How to maintain equilibrium and a positive, creative atmosphere.

  • How to facilitate problem solving.

  • How to manage a classroom—including arranging the space to support art activities and managing logistics and behavior.

  • How to collaborate with teachers and other Teaching Artists.

  • How to establish relationships with other staff in an art education setting.

  • How to conduct an art inquiry.

  • How to organize a class trip to an art venue.

  • How to organize a presentation of completed art works to appropriate audiences.

  • How to document art making process and finished works.

  • How to document teaching and learning processes.

  • Keep all stakeholders’ telephone numbers in our cell phones.

  • Current educational terminology in common use where one is teaching.

  • Terminology used among people of varying abilities.

  • It helps to know a second language, especially one commonly used in the community where you teach.

  • Yourself—you must know what your own limitations are, what you can handle, when to say no.

What have you learned is the most important thing a Teaching Artist needs to be able to do?

 

A Teaching Artist needs to be able to assess a situation quickly and calmly and come up with potential solutions to unexpected challenges such as:

  • Unexpected “important” visitors arrive in the middle of your class and want an explanation of what you’re doing.

  • You get a new student when the art project is half over.

  • You don’t have enough materials, or space or time for the lesson.

  • Half of the class is absent on an important day for the project.

  • The students finish early and need something else to do.

  • A student is unhappy with their artwork and wants to destroy it or leave.

  • Damaged artwork.

  • Project is not working out the way you expected.

  • No access to usual art classroom.

  • Sudden cancellation of a class.

  • Injury of a student.

  • Conflict between students.

  • Conflict with a teacher or other staff member.

  • Being unexpectedly delayed on the way to class.

What or who has been your best teacher?

 

The students have been my consistent best teachers. They always let me know how I am doing through the things they say and the art they create. Ongoing reflection on my teaching supports this as well.

 

What is the most important advice you would give to a young artist about to enter the field?

 

Try to work in the setting and with the age groups that you feel most comfortable with at first. Have fun with your students. Look for opportunities to observe more experienced Teaching Artists and discuss/question what you see them doing. Remember that when you choose to become a Teaching Artist you are also making a financial choice. For the most part, your jobs are temporary or seasonal, come without benefits or health insurance and are generally not that well paid. Don’t do this unless you love it!

 

Has there been one experience that immediately comes into your mind that changed the way you teach?

           

My teaching has evolved and grown over time. Each year I have added a new trick or skill, or changed something that didn’t work in a way that is responsive to the way I see that my students are learning and to the feedback I receive from others. I have been fortunate to participate in many professional development activities over the past twenty-five years that made it possible to refine my teaching skills using the best practices of others. I have also learned many techniques from classroom teachers that I have worked with in collaborative programs. All these things I have incorporated in an organic way to develop my teaching as it currently stands. In my most recent professional development, that of working with students of varying abilities, I have learned new levels of flexibility and ways to facilitate students’ learning through making accommodations on an individual basis. This is a never-ending process.

 

What sustained (s) your practice as a Teaching Artist?

 

My practice as a Teaching Artist is sustained first by hugs, cheers, applause, smiles and just the sheer joy the students express at having the opportunity to make art. Then it is sustained by my own making my own artwork because I can arrange my life so that I spend time in the studio when I am not teaching. It is also sustained by the confidence I have in my ability to do it well and in the pleasure I take in doing it. Then it is sustained by my relationships with the people I work with: the administrations, the other Teaching Artists, the classroom teachers, and the staffs of the organizations I work with.
