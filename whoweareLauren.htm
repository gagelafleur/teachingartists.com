LAUREN ATKINSON

WASHINGTON (Seattle)		  
   

Photo by Susie Fitzhugh

Lauren Atkinson is a founding member and master Teaching Artist with Arts Corps, an arts education program in Seattle, Washington established in 2000. She has served as Faculty Development Manager from 2005-2010, and in this role she worked closely with a faculty of thirty Teaching Artists from multiple disciplines, overseeing curriculum and professional development. She has worked with many arts education programs in the Pacific Northwest including Seattle Public Schools, Arts Impact, Principals Project, Centrum Education, the Seattle Center Academy, the Seattle Children’s Museum and Power of Hope. Her creative practice has evolved and developed over a 20-year period as a collaborative visual artist and Teaching Artist working with people of all ages and backgrounds. Lauren Atkinson’s work is grounded in the power of learning by doing. Her role as a teacher is that of a “bridge,” extending her hand to support each individual’s own way of being and seeing.  She believes each of us learns in a unique way, and she emphasizes the dynamic reciprocal relationship between teacher and student. 

 

Photo by Susie Fitzhugh
 

What or who inspired you to become a Teaching Artist?

 

When I was in my final semester in high school, I applied and was accepted to attend a visual arts camp for "gifted" students at Centrum in Washington State. The experience of learning with Teaching Artists was a transformative experience for me. I knew then that it was a way of life that I wanted to be part of.

 

What has been most helpful to you in your work?

 

Working with a very supportive arts organization, Arts Corps. The organization gave me the opportunity to learn, to grow, and to practice my own pedagogy as a Teaching Artist. Arts Corps has created and found work for me in many venues and has supported my vision as an educator.

 

Arts Corps was created and developed by Teaching Artists. The organization has a deep understanding of what it means to be a Teaching Artist and has been willing to grow and change as the Teaching Artists have deepened their practice over the years. As an organization it values and integrates the voice and the needs of the youth and the communities it serves. It believes that education, and especially access to quality arts education, is a social justice issue. Arts Corps has made a commitment to educate and train staff and the board to recognize their position within the power structure of the institutions they work with and how to better advocate for change to meet the needs of the diverse youth and community it serves. In addition, Arts Corps is has become an ally organization that works with other community organizations to create equal access and support for education and the many needs of the communities in the Seattle, King County area.

 

What have you learned are the unique talents of a Teaching Artist?

 

I have learned to surf the chaos of a vibrant learning environment. Students who are engaged and passionate about their learning can be highly energetic. I have learned not to be fearful of this "big" creative energy. I have learned to harness it and use it as an opportunity to reflect back to the students all of the many ways we learn. I have learned to share with students the power in quiet, also, as another place to learn and not be fearful of it.

 

My pedagogy is informed by listening very carefully to my students and to myself. I trust the journey that we begin and travel along and practice the Arts Corps Creative Habits: imagining possibilities, persistence and discipline, courage and risk taking, critical thinking, and reflection. I teach to the spirit of the student. Their age is of less importance to me than who they are in the moment of creative practice. I understand where students may be developmentally, but I also understand that this is on a continuum. I have received great insight and words of wisdom from a five year-old student as well as from a student who is fifteen. We all have so much to share no matter how old we are, and it is all equally valid when we are engaged in lifelong creative learning. This understanding is the root system of my pedagogy.

 

What have you learned that a Teaching Artist needs to know?

 

I learned from my early experience working as an after-school caregiver. This experience provided me with great skills for the classroom and behavior management.

 

Cultivate and feed your own practice, this will guide you well if you listen carefully and get out  of your own way. Think of your self as a student. You are always learning, and more often than not your students will take you on a creative journey that they are driving and you are supporting with your experience and practice. Having fun is essential too!

 

Draw forth and support the way in which students see and experience the world they live in to help them; Help them build confidence in their own knowing, and what they can continue to learn from their lives directly from their "internal loci"; Experience living as a creative practice. We are here to better understand ourselves living in community with love and compassion with all our relationships and with the nations we share this beautiful earth.

 

What have you learned is the most important thing a Teaching Artists needs to be able to do?

 

To be flexible and to be a practicing collaborator. Be a listener, listen more and be observant, craft this as your practice. Practice what you preach. Model this by walking your talk.

 

What or who has been your best teacher?

 

My students are my best teachers.

 

What is the most important advice you would give to a young artist about to enter the field?

 

Spend time volunteering and working with youth to see if it is something you feel you have the ability to take on with care, without ego, and to be ready to learn daily about yourself and your own short comings. Also, look at why you think you have something to give back, or what it means to work with "at risk youth". It is very important to be firmly established in your own practice as an artist and your path with your profession.

 

Steady work and pay are the most challenging part of being a Teaching Artist. Most of us are subcontractors working in a world where many folks ask if you volunteer your time to do this work. There seems to be a "mythic" belief that the artist has so much to give, and we live off air!

 

Steady sometimes, sometimes not, sometimes with too much teaching you can become too dependent financially and let your own work diminish. It is a delicate balance that needs constant attention. Aging is another factor, especially in regard to healthcare, families, and planning for later years. The social safety net is thin. Making it as an artist in the USA is a challenge to say the least, overwhelming at times that takes many of us out!

 

Has there been one experience that immediately comes into your mind that changed the way you teach?

 

I don't think I have had one experience, but there is a continuous thread that keeps revealing itself, and that is the issue of “adultism” and its effect on the creativity of youth. I find that teachers and care giving staff so often are more concerned for their own needs and do not value or really understand the importance of creative learning. The need for behavior management and the fear of chaotic energy overrides the creative needs of their students. In regard to my teaching, I have embraced an emergent curriculum approach, paying attention to the interests of the students, and supporting the learning experience from this place. I find I have to listen well, and put my own "teacherly" needs and preconceived ideas aside to be able to see what is surfacing. I find when I am true to this approach the students learn to trust the process and great work can come from this place. Having fun is essential to this process, and much learning can happen through joyful play. 

 

What is  your classroom experience encapsulates your thoughts on the work of a Teaching Artist?

 

Working as a Teaching Artist is challenging, I have found working in the schools and in after school programs that there are some that don't take you seriously as an educator, and the nomadic teaching style over time can be exhausting (especially as a visual artist). I have found that I have to be a strong compassionate warrior, gently mentoring youth and adults, to remember the healing and nourishing qualities of play that feed the creative imagination.
