CECIL MCDONALD, JR.
ILLINOIS (Chicago)		  
   

Self Portrait

 		 Through photography, video, and text, Cecil McDonald, Jr. explores the intersections of masculinity, familial relations, and the artistic and intellectual pursuits of black culture. McDonald studied fashion, house music and dance club culture before receiving a MFA in Photography at Columbia College Chicago, where he currently serves as an adjunct professor and a Teaching Artist at the Center for Community Arts Partnership at Columbia College Chicago. His work has been exhibited both nationally and internationally, with works in the permanent collection of The Cleveland Museum of Art, Chicago Bank of America LaSalle Collection, and the Harris Bank Collection. He was awarded the: Joyce Foundation Midwest Voices & Visions Award, the Artadia Award, The Swiss Benevolent Society, Lucerne, Switzerland, Residency and the 3Arts Teaching Artist Award. McDonald Jr.’s recent projects include Looking for Baldwin: A Stranger in the Village, and I Can’t Turnaround: Where do we go from Here?—a Teaching Artist residency using photography and text to explore questions and affects of intensive, intervention initiatives in underachieving Chicago Public Schools commonly referred to as School Turnaround. 

 


5th grade student from Theodore Herzl Elementary School,
Chicago Public Schools

 

What or who inspired you to become a Teaching Artist?

 

Good question but I’m afraid there was no one who I saw as a model, I fell backwards into this career.

 

What has been most helpful to you in your work?

 

I would have to say my interest in my own art practice and a very real interest in children and young people.

 

What have you learned are the unique talents of a Teaching Artist?

 

I have to say a sense of humor, not sarcasm mind you but the ability to see the absurdity in a situation and use it in a Teaching Artist practice.

 

What have you learned that a Teaching Artist needs to know?

 

In addition to humor I would have to say a healthy obsession with what young people are looking at, the technology they use, not that you want to become like them but you take these references and run them through your process and practice as an artist, then hand it back to the students in your care.

 

What have you learned is the most important thing a Teaching Artists needs to be able to do?

 

Mentor, have a sense of humor, and be yourself at all times, the students can smell inauthentic sensibilities.

 

What or who has been your best teacher?

 

Avery R. Young, Cynthia Weiss, and my students

 

What is the most important advice you would give to a young artist about to enter the field?

 

You are a mentor first and foremost; use what is relevant in a student's life, as you filter it through your art practice. This is not a democratic system. It comes from the artist. However the artist allows plenty of room for the process to include the students’ ideas and experiences.

 

Has there been one experience that immediately comes into your mind that changed the way you teach?

 

I did a project about fears and expectations; I started by first talking extensively about my own. This made students open up more quickly, in order to get down to the business of art making.

 

What is your classroom experience encapsulates your thoughts on the work of a Teaching Artist?

 

Every residency is different but I try to think of the classroom as my studio and the students are my collaborators. I try to blur the lines between the studio and the classroom.

 

What sustained (s) your practice as a Teaching Artist?

 

My own practice, students respond to you if there is a sense that they are NOT the only subjects on the teacher’s mind, that the teacher can bring outside experiences and yes achievements from the outside world into the often-artificial world of the classroom.
