REGGIE COLEMAN
CALIFORNIA (Los Angeles) 		  
   

Photo by Mary Whalen
 

Reggie Coleman has been working with the Community Arts Partnership (CAP) program since 1996.  His association with CAP began while he was a student at California Institute for the Arts. Reggie is a graduate of U.C. Berkeley with dual degrees in both Mass Communications and Dramatic Art.  He also holds a Masters of Fine Arts degree in directing for theatre, video and cinema from CalArts.  Presently, he works as an art instructor, freelance editor, and educator with the Los Angeles Unified School District (LAUSD). Since 2000, Reggie has also served as a site coordinator and instructor at Self Help Graphics and Art, with the CAP Digital Media Program.  In this groundbreaking venture, teenagers from the East Los Angeles community are provided with training in digital and visual arts. Reggie also serves as both a video and printmaking instructor in the ArtsCOOL program, sponsored by CAP and LAUSD’s Arts Education Branch.  In this program, local artists teach students from Options High Schools a variety of art forms.  Reggie has been an ArtsCOOL instructor since the program’s inception in the spring of 2003. In addition, Reggie is a business partner with Tilford Art Group, in charge of publicity and educational development.

 

Photo by Mary Whalen


 

What or who inspired you to become a Teaching Artist?

 

My initial inspiration to become a Teaching Artist came form my work as an acting instructor for Dream West, a program for at-risk children in the West Oakland area of Northern California.   The acting workshop would culminate with a performance of scenes and vignettes written by myself, other staff members, as well as the students.  At the time, I was in college earning duel degrees in Dramatic Art and Communications at the University of California, Berkeley.  For three years, I would look forward to this summer job, as it was both a challenging and rewarding experience.  It was in this job that I first realized that not only did I enjoy teaching youth, but I also became aware of the fact that sharing my skills and life experiences with the young artists was very appealing to me.  As they would often come to watch me acting in productions on campus, these students not only got to know me as their theater instructor, but they also got to see me in action, on stage in various productions.  I realized that this type of working relationship, over the years, was very effective on how my students viewed me in relation to the craft of acting.  My students saw me as an individual who was not only their instructor, but also a role model who was actively pursuing my goals in the area of arts that I happened to be teaching.

 

While in college, I also met Dr. Margaret B. Wilkerson, a woman who influenced and enriched my interest in not only the arts, but she also piqued my curiosity in the possibility of becoming a Teaching Artist.  Dr. Wilkerson, who was head of the African American Studies Department at Berkeley, was not only my professor in several acting courses, but she directed two productions that I acted in and co-wrote, during my undergraduate career.  Dr. Wilkerson became my mentor, but moreover she was also the key player in my realizing that the work I did as an aspiring actor and a developing playwright was important.  She served as a prime example that one could work in education, while continuing to work as a successful artist.  Today Dr. Wilkerson serves as serves as Director of Media, Arts and Culture at the Ford Foundation.  She still provides guidance and mentorship in my life as an artist, as well as career advice as an art educator.

 

What has been most helpful to you in your work?

 

My experience as an educator with over 25 years of experience has proven to be most helpful to me in my work.  I have not only taught various art forms in the classroom including filmmaking, acting, digital media and visual art, but I have also worked many years with the Los Angeles Unified School District as an educator in various elementary, middle and high schools. 

 

My work in arts education has also been edified by my experience as an instructor with the CalArts Community Arts Partnership (CAP) program. I started working with CAP as a graduate student while working on my MFA degree in directing for theater, video and cinema at CalArts.  This program has allowed me the opportunity to work with prominent art and community organizations such as Self Help Graphics & Art, Plaza De La Raza, KAOS Network, and Inner City Arts. 

 

What have you learned are the unique talents of a Teaching Artist?

 

Over the years I have honed my skills in regards to how to establish an effective working relationship in which my students trust me enough to be open and free in their work.  The ultimate goal is that they contribute ideas in class discussions and create projects, without the fear of judgment or harsh criticism.  That said, as an art instructor, I feel that it is also important to develop the talent to impart constructive criticism without stifling the creativity of your students. A betrayal of trust and respect in the culture of a classroom, that the instructor and students have established, can destroy the sense of candor that you have all worked so hard to develop.  I usually start the year by coming up with a list of ground rules for the class.  These ground rules are generated from the class in a group discussion.  The list is then clarified and agreed upon by the group.  The students, instructor and teaching assistants, then sign the ground rules, as a written contract.  I have found that this process helps establish an environment in which students can be creative and honest, with some form of established structure.

 

What have you learned that a Teaching Artist needs to know?

 

It might sound a bit cliché, but in my many years of teaching, I have found that an art instructor can learn just as much from his or her students as they learn from the instructor.  As an artist and as an instructor, you never stop growing and learning new lessons. Because they have their fingers on the pulse of important issues that face our society today, my young students often keep me informed on the relevant issues that they grapple with both locally and globally.  Astonishingly, it is often these same issues that I decide to explore in my own work.  The insight of my students is often very candid and crystal clear.  Their opinions are very definitive, with little or no gray areas.  Their viewpoints on societal norms that baffle many adults are not overly affected with conjecture based on the fear of how they might be perceived by their peers.  As educators, especially in the arts, we need to acknowledge the viewpoints and opinions of our youth. 

 

What have you learned is the most important thing a Teaching Artist needs to be able to do?

 

First and foremost, in my opinion, a Teaching Artist needs to be able to find a balance between his or her work as an artist and his/her job as an art educator.  Time management is key to success in both areas.  The Teaching Artist is essentially balancing two careers, one as an artist and the other as an educator.  In the event, it is so easy to neglect one for the other.  When I take on a job, I strive to commit myself wholeheartedly to that position.  I have found it essential to set aside a few hours each day to focus on my work as an artist, as it is so easy to fall into the trap of focusing all of my time on planning and preparation for my classes.  Total neglect for one or the other, can result in an individual feeling frustrated or unfulfilled.  So to avoid this common pitfall, I consciously put myself on a weekly, and sometimes daily schedule.

 

What or who has been your best teacher?

 

Experience has been one of my best teachers.  Through both trial and error, my years in the classroom have taught me how to become a better educator and artist.  My biggest mistakes have without doubt been my best lessons on what I need to alter in my teaching style or pedagogy.  Through self-evaluation and analysis, I always pinpoint where I went wrong.  I then consider an alternative approach for future lessons.

 

I’ve learned that is extremely important to be very honest with my students.  Sometimes lessons that I have planned out don’t always go over well with them. Being open to change and allowing myself to be flexible to modify my agenda is what grounds me in the eyes of my students.  I very candidly let my students know that I often learn just as much from them, as they learn from me.   In their projects, my students commonly explore some of the same issues in their work as I did as a young, developing artist.  Therefore, I can save my students some heartache, by revealing how I dealt with the same issues and obstacles. Through anecdotes and by revealing my past experiences, I can give my students a blueprint of how I dealt with similar issues and subject matter.  I find that this type of dialogue with my students is key to helping them gain knowledge from similar life lessons that I have experienced.  

 

What is the most important advice you would give to a young artist about to enter the field?

 

I would tell young artists, about to enter the field of teaching in the arts, to remain open.  Not only should they be willing to alter their lesson plans to accommodate the needs of their students, but they should also stay open to ideas of the youth, our future leaders and educators.  It is so easy to become stubborn or hardened by the struggles associated with burgeoning careers in both the arts and education.  Nonetheless, it is essential and much more fulfilling to not fight change, which is inevitable, but rather welcome it gracefully by opening their perspective to unknown viewpoints.

 

Has there been one experience that immediately comes into your mind that changed the way you teach? 

 

Several experiences come to mind in relation to changing the manner in which I teach.  Throughout the years numerous students have attributed their association with me or their involvement in one of my classes to the art related paths that they have chosen to pursue in their respective lives.  In fact, several former acting students from my classes at Dream West (the program in West Oakland) have reached out to me to let me know that they attribute the career choices that they have made in life to something that I have said, or through their association with me.  This is why I teach. 

 

I have suffered discontentment in the profession, and there have been days when I wonder if all that I do is in vain.  Sadly, over the years, I have lost several students to the violence commonly associated with life in urban environments.  A few of these students were profoundly talented individuals who could have seen very lucrative professions in the arts.  But due to happenstance or their association with gangs, their lights were snuffed out far too early.  This can be very frustrating as an educator.  However, to know that those who did make it out of violent situations, with even a meager bit of help or motivation on my part, gives me some form of solace and satisfaction.  Whether by way of their drive to achieve greater, or through their pursuit of higher education, I am content with the fact that they are chasing their dreams.  By far, the rewards of being a Teaching Artist outweigh my doubts, frustrations or issues with inadequate financial compensation.

 

What in your classroom experience encapsulates your thoughts on the work of a Teaching Artist? 

 

I think that the adage, “An essential aspect of creativity is not being afraid to fail,” is an appropriate comment that comes to mind.  This idea of being fearless, not only is my mantra as an artist, but also serves as the basis of my work as an art educator.

 

What sustained (s) your practice as a Teaching Artist?

 

Financially, working as a freelance artist and filmmaker, coupled with my work as a teacher with both CAP and LAUSD, have afforded me to make a living as a Teaching Artist.  However, the greater motivating factor that has sustained my interest in working in arts education is derived from the fact that one’s work is never done.  There is always another young mind that I can influence, through my work as an artist, or through a lesson that I impart to one of my students as their instructor.  Through my work in the classroom, I, like my students, continue to learn and grow.  Thus, a healthy symbiotic relationship is created that never ceases to affect me as a teacher, and influences me as an artist.
