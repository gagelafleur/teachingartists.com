SOJN BOOTHROYD
MICHIGAN (Kalamazoo) 		  
   

Photo by Mary Whalen
 

Sojn Boothroyd is an educator, social justice activist, and award-winning interdisciplinary artist with twenty years of experience as an arts education practitioner and guest artist in K-12 schools. Her professional experience ranges from organizational leadership to research and teaching, including training Teaching Artists and in-school teachers in cities across the U.S. She is a published author, holds an Ed.M. in Arts in Education from the Harvard Graduate School of Education, and has worked for Project Zero. She has also worked for three organizations that have received the National Arts and Humanities Youth Program Awards from the President’s Office for outstanding after-school and out-of-school programs: Seattle’s Arts Corps, Street-Level Youth Media, and Marwen in Chicago. Currently, Sojn Boothroyd is a faculty member at Northeastern University and Western Michigan University, as well as the creator of an arts-integrated bias-based project that takes an intersectional approach to addressing discriminatory practices, such as racism, transphobia, and homophobia in K-12 schools. She is also the director and Teaching Artist for a youth-led PhotoVoice project on the relationship between sexual health and racism. The project is being brought to an international public health conference in South Africa.

What or who inspired you to become a Teaching Artist?

 

  1. Youth. I love working with youth through an authentic creative process—I find the work rewarding, fun, and challenging. I am inspired by their excitement, inspiration, unique voices, ideas, breakthroughs, and realizations of their capabilities that often resonate out to other areas in their life. To put it simply: we have a great time together.

  2. Organizations that I worked for which supported my work as an artist, gave me freedom and flexibility, offered me new opportunities, and paid me sustainable wages that uplifted what teaching artistry was and could be.

  3. There have been many educators, social workers, and artists who have influenced me throughout my life. I am not sure if I would have made it through high school without the arts and the freedom I found in the creative experiences led by the artist-educators that I had the opportunity to work with and know. My teachers from Velocity Dance Studio in Seattle, out artist-educators and straight allies who supported me in my process of developing as an artist who served as a role model for me and expanded my perception of what is possible for myself. More recently, the professors I had the opportunity to study with through the Arts in Education program at the Harvard Graduate School of Education.

  4. Ongoing realizations that I have had over the years, throughout my own process of teaching and learning as to the relationship between creativity, learning, and social justice. Practicing the ability to imagine different ideas is critical to solving and transforming real-life problems. Social movements, for example, first have to be imagined as possible, before they can ever become a reality. The imagining of ideas or possibilities is necessary for liberation to occur—and through that creative process we are actively participating in the creative construction and transformation of the world around us.

 

I have come to see authentic creative thinking and art-making as an act of freedom—a profound vehicle to self-authoring and owning the way we think, see, and create ourselves, perceiving what is possible in our own lives and in the world around us.

 

What has been most helpful to you in your work?

What have you learned that a Teaching Artist needs to know?

What is the most important advice you would give to a young artist about to enter the field?

 

    1.   Proactive Planning

 

A.    I meet with the facility liaison and/or classroom teacher (depending on the residency type) in advance, to learn more about the youth I will be working with and to see the space where I will be teaching. I discuss the protocol for emergencies, and for how I am going to work with the facility partner. If I am traveling a long distance to the residency location, and we are not able to meet in advance, I gather as much information as possible, through phone, email, or Skype. This helps inform my residency and lesson plans.

 

B.     I have a clear plan for how I am going to manage the class as a whole along with individual needs and behaviors. Effective teaching artistry requires a delicate balance of keeping the class interested in the learning and creative process, while simultaneously managing individual needs and/or behaviors that can sometimes be disruptive, and/or hurtful to others.

 

Ultimately, a well-prepared, fun, and adaptive lesson plan—along with a teaching methodology that is engaging, empowering, and socially just—prevents many classroom and behavior management challenges. How I manage the classroom is intricately woven into my lesson plan.

 

    2.   Residency Design and Lesson Planning

 

A.    I develop an overarching plan for the residency (an outline for the content flow of classes for the residency period): if there is a final youth performance after 8 weeks of classes, what do they need to be working on at week 4, or week 6, in order to have a successful performance?

  1.  

B.     Then I design daily lesson plans. Some elements I consider when designing a lesson plan:
 

  • Engaging multiple types of learners—kinesthetic, visual, and auditory.

  • Differentiated instruction, so that I can coach youth individually and in small groups, letting them create, learn, and grow at a pace and in a manner that serves them best.

  • Weaving in exercises that develop the social-emotional culture of the class, building trust amongst classmates and emotional safety, necessary for young people to feel comfortable taking creative risks.

  • Diversifying the learning experience and process: I lead the entire class through specific activities, and also have the youth work with partners, individually, and in small groups on collaborative projects.

  • Setting weekly opportunities for the youth to share the work they are creating with their classmates, so they can learn from each other, have a weekly goal to meet, and take steps to build their confidence as they prepare for a larger showcase or performance.

  • Challenging them to explore creative possibilities through guided exercises—to think deeply, creatively, critically, to solve problems—to listen, observe and to learn from their peers.

  • Including multiple methods for group reflection and discussion. 

 

3.   Working With The Youth To Cultivate A Socially Just, Learner-Centered Classroom.

 

Establishing this social-emotional culture, on the very first day of classes, is critical. As part of this process, I work with the youth to create shared-ownership/shared responsibility for the class and the work that is created. One of the ways I go about this, for example:

 

On the first day of class, I have the youth brainstorm a list of agreements that they think are important to set, in order for them to be able to work well together—through a creative and collaborative process. They come up with ideas and rules that establish the architecture for an empowering learning and creative process for everyone.

 

4.   Considering How I Teach

 

I am always in the process of developing my teaching methodology. Things I think about when I teach include:

 

A.    Reflecting, questioning, and analyzing myself—especially in regard to my power and privileges—and examining this in the context of my role as “teacher.”

B.     Thinking of myself as a listener and facilitator—as the person responsible for coaching youth individually and in groups.

C.     Following the learners’ processes, their ideas and wisdom, and adapting to them, so the learners get to be the leaders, directors, and creators of their collaboratively created artistic work. I coach and give them guidance as needed.

D.    Focusing on the wisdom of the learners, not the right answers.

E.     Creating an environment where there is no right answer, or not one right answer—but a multitude of possibilities.

F.      Creating an experience that is learner-centered and intrinsically motivating for youth.

G.    Weaving in skill-building exercises and creative games that are inherently fun.

H.    Being flexible, adaptive, and willing to throw my lesson plan out the door if the youth need to go in a different direction. After working with youth through the arts for twenty years, I can still say that every group is different.

 

I ALWAYS bring in a set of alternative ideas, creativity exercises, so that I can adapt to where the group is going, or needs to go.

 

I ALWAYS revise the lesson plan for my next day of class, based on the class that day.

 

5.   Other Tips

 

A.    The first day is the most critical for establishing the culture of the class.

B.     Keep learning: listen and learn from the youth, talk to other Teaching Artists and school teachers, attend professional development workshops and conferences—not just locally, but also nationally and internationally if you can.

C.     Read and study arts and social justice education philosophy, pedagogical theory, research, and advocacy.
