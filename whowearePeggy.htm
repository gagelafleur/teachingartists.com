PEGGY HASEGAWA
CALIFORNIA (Los Angeles) 		  
   


 

My name is Peggy Hasegawa.  I have been a Teaching Artist for almost thirty years.  (It amazes me to say that!)  It is a privilege, a pleasure, and a passion to do what I do. 

I am an “independent contractor,” so life is kind of precarious.   But over the years, my teaching experiences have led to me to teach for the Los Angeles County Museum Of Art, UCLA Extension, The Los Angeles County Music Center, Education Division, The Segerstrom Center For The Arts In Orange County, The Skirball Cultural Center In Los Angeles, The Hollywood Bowl Summer Sounds Program, and other museums, organizations, and at various festivals.  I also teach professional development workshops.

My students are ages 3 ½ to over 80 years old.  Besides teaching in the museums, I teach in schools through various organizations.

My special interests are at-risk youth and people with special needs.  I do work for the Department Of Mental Health and For L.A. County Probation, in a probation camp.  I have volunteered in one of the L.A. housing projects. 

I want to live a conscious life.   I want to be a positive voice.

 

 

Passion

 

Have a passion for what you are teaching, a passion to teach, a passion to learn, a passion to connect with your students.

 

Prepare

 

Research, study what you love.

 

We work hard to prepare and present lessons and projects that will excite, inspire, and delight your students. It is exciting to have an idea in your head, and then develop it into a workable project/experience for your students.   However, you may have the best project planned, but if you have not planned the best way to execute it, if you cannot explain the process clearly, step by step for your students, they will not feel comfortable or successful.

 

Preparing the materials is huge.  Testing different materials, searching for the right materials, prepping them for the project.  A lot more time prepping than the time you spend in the classroom.

 

Be flexible.  Think on your feet, because things always come up while you’re teaching.   Students will have an interesting question or comment.   How can you incorporate their contributions into the conversation?  Someone will be struggling with something: How can you help him or her find his or her way.  A lot of this is teaching experience you will gain along the way. 

 

Persevere

 

To get work?   Passion, prepare, persevere.  

 

If you truly want to be a Teaching Artist, passion rises above everything else, the financial hardships, the disappointments, the really, really hard work it takes.  You are a Teaching Artist because that’s the only thing you want to be.  If you discover all of this is not your “cup of tea”, stop look for your passion.

 

Prepare.  Hone your skills and your reputation.  “Good enough” is not good enough. Prepare well-thought-out ideas and plans when talking to a prospective employer.  But realize that you may have to come up with new ideas on the spot.

 

Persevere.  Never give up. If you work on skills and have a good work ethic, someone will give you a chance.  Then you build on that.  Sometimes, the chance comes totally out of the blue.     Thomas Edison said,  “Opportunity is missed by most people because it is dressed in overalls and looks like work.” 

 

If you need to find jobs, so you can survive as a Teaching Artist, other work could fuel your creativity and present other opportunities, if you are open.

 

Being a Teaching Artist is a lot of work.  But it’s the best work in the world.   

 

Thomas Edison said. “ I never did a day’s work in my life.  It was all fun.”

 

I am a visual artist, so my students and I get to learn and experience everything from Matisse to Mexican paper cutting, from Klee to clay, from origami to handmade books. 

 

But, truly, more than that, Teaching Artists get the chance to help people find something good and beautiful inside themselves.  And when they find that, you wish for them to continue discovering their potential so whether it’s a child in a classroom or a youth in a probation camp, you want them to create a positive, creative energy to sustain them to grow and go forward.  All of us are creative, all of our lives.
