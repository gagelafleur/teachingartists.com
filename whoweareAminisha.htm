AMINISHA FERDINAND
LOUISIANA (New Orleans) 		  
   


 

Aminisha Ferdinand is the Arts Integration Instructional Coach for KID smART’s Model Schools Initiative. She joined the KID smART team as an independent Teaching Artist before transitioning into the role of Arts Coach, facilitating arts integration workshops for classroom teachers, coaching KID smART Teaching Artists, and serving as artist-in-residence at Langston Hughes Academy. She travels throughout the south, facilitating workshops for teachers and artists on effective theater integrated learning.  Her previous experience as an educator includes a drama faculty position at an Atlanta middle school and residencies in community and school-based programs in New York and New Orleans.  While attaining her Masters Degree, Aminisha studied briefly at the Center for the Theatre of the Oppressed in Rio de Janeiro.  Following, she worked with Invisible Children in Gulu, Uganda, partnering with a local classroom teacher to integrate arts and participatory activities into student lessons. She has served on the board of Crossroads Institute and Theatrix, a graduate student-run theater company.  Aminisha received her BFA in Theater Performance from the University of Miami. She has an MA in Educational Theatre from NYU.  She occasionally performs in the storytelling showcase, Shipwrecked in Nola, at the New Movement Theater.

 


 

What or who inspired you to become a Teaching Artist?

 

I didn’t intentionally decide to become a Teaching Artist.  I was living in Los Angeles and felt like my pursuit of an acting career in Los Angeles was not fulfilling. I come from a family of activists and discovered that they had rooted in me a deeper responsibility to be of contribution to my community than I realized. I loved the theater, but not the business of promoting a career as an actor so I sought out information on MFA programs in directing with the plan to work with young people in theater. Turns out, you need to submit a directing portfolio for MFA programs and since I had not directed before, I began working with a couple of youth theaters in my hometown of New Orleans. The hurricane hit a week later and my family moved to Atlanta.  My sister had substitute taught before and applied to Atlanta Public Schools for substitute teaching. When they called her in for fingerprinting, I went with her and signed up also. Took an assignment a month later and at the end of my first school day, the principal asked me what else I do and ended up offering me my own drama class.

 

I stumbled through as best I could, researching how to write a syllabus and lesson plans online and pulling from theater activities I had learned in my arts high school.  Like most beginning teachers, I didn’t know what I was doing, but I knew even less because I had never taken an education class.  It was hard. I cried a lot and felt like a failure even more often, but the small successes I saw in the middle schoolers as they memorized tongue twisters, wrote skits, improvised scenes, it motivated me to want to learn how to do this better.  Through another set of circumstances divinely laid out for me, I discovered the MA program in Education Theater at NYU and started my Teaching Artist journey.

 

What has been most helpful to you in your work?

  • Training and then opportunities to implement the training – in a continuous cycle – so that I am always learning and implementing.  I’ve been doing this now for seven years, and I still seek out learning opportunities and collaborative communities with other teachers who are looking to grow in their pedagogy.

  • A strong foundation in pedagogy of the oppressed and reflections on my privilege to inform how I plan lessons, what lessons I plan, how I teach, what I teach, how I discipline.

  • Reading and studying about what is happening in the field of education.

  • Finding an organization where the people in it match my teaching philosophy.  I could get caught up in product or caught up in just being creative, and miss the balance of what kids need.

What have you learned are the unique talents of a Teaching Artist?

 

A combined understanding of the art form and the art and science of teaching.  A Teaching Artist has invested the time and energy in growth as an educator and an artist.  A Teaching Artist has the unique mindset of an artist – valuing the individual perspective, recognizing and teaching from a lens of expressing one’s ideas confidently and communicating effectively, instead of what’s right or wrong.  The values of an artist are ideally the values of a teacher, but can get lost when a teacher enters into the system of schooling. A Teaching Artist has the unique blessing of being outside of that school system mindset and can bring a fresher perspective to teaching.

 

What have you learned that a Teaching Artist needs to know?

 

A Teaching Artist needs to know:

  • How to create a student-centered learning experience.

  • How to assess that learning experience.

  • Their teaching philosophy, and how it aligns with their teaching practice in reality.

  • How to talk about the effectiveness of their work.

  • The power of reflection – for themselves and for their students as learners: make the time to facilitate it

  • How helpful it is to find a community of teachers and Teaching Artists that will challenge you in your practice and views and support you when the work becomes taxing.

  • That you can do a disservice to children in schools when you radically change their classroom culture for the 45 minutes you are there without creating a way for them to transition back into that classroom environment before you leave.

  • That their job is to help empower students to express their learning through the arts, not only to teach them how to create or perform in the arts.

What have you learned is the most important thing a Teaching Artists needs to be able to do?

 

Respond to what is happening in the moment and adjust

 

What or who has been your best teacher?

 

Time/maturity, experience, and thoughtful people (all in collusion)

 

What is the most important advice you would give to a young artist about to enter the field?

 

Volunteer at a community center or church with a small group of students.  Find out on the first day what they are like doing and what they’d like to learn about.  Make an overall plan with big goals.  Keep a journal and reflect on what worked and didn’t work after each session.

 

Has there been one experience that immediately comes into your mind that changed the way you teach?

 

No, my teaching is truly a composite of time, experience, conversation, collaboration, and training.

 

What is your classroom experience encapsulates your thoughts on the work of a Teaching Artist?

 

My kids use the language of theater arts when they talk to me and to each other.  If their teacher hasn’t elected to have me as a partner in their classroom but the student was with me last year, they will ask, “How come we don’t have acting anymore?” or say, “I remember you from kindergarten. We pantomimed.”  Teachers tell me how they’ve discovered ways in for students that they struggled with behavioral disruptions. 

 

What sustained (s) your practice as a Teaching Artist?

 

Finding an organization that I felt I could grow with. Finding a school that I was able to continue in residency with for multiple consecutive years.  Students expressing success every year I worked with them.  Teachers expressing appreciation for the work I was doing with their students.  I was really blessed to have things come into alignment for me, where I was able to get support for the work I was doing.  But what really sustains me is the joy and growth I see in my students as they learn and express themselves through the arts.  I feel so strongly that the arts engage student thinking and expression in ways that they are being oppressed and suppressed in our current schooling focus that although I am not able to always teach in the schools in a way that I feel is fully in alignment with my teaching philosophy, I am able to create opportunities for young people to create successes in learning and for teachers to discover intelligences in their students they didn’t realize were present.  In looking back at my first ever-teaching job in Atlanta, my successes definitely weren’t more present than my perceived failures, but the power of those moments of student joy and pride through theater, overwhelmed the ever-present struggles.  Now that I’ve gotten training and found communities of support and a supportive organization that values life-long learning, I see no end to the work that I can do through the partnership of teaching and the arts.
