KARLA NWEJE
MINNESOTA (Minneapolis)		  
   



 		 Karla Nweje has a background in concert dance, theater, literary arts, and education.  She holds a BFA in Dance Performance & Composition from Brooklyn College/CUNY.  She is a published author and contributing writer to literary journals and anthologies.  After obtaining teaching credentials, Ms. Nweje worked as a NYC high school language arts teacher by day and a performer by night.  During this time, she intuitively used theater arts techniques to enhance the learning process in her classroom. After moving to Atlanta, GA, Ms. Nweje served as a dance specialist in two public high schools and taught for dance studios and outreach programs while continuing to choreograph, perform, and write.  Her subsequent work as an Atlanta Teaching Artist and arts/youth development coordinator largely targeted under-served populations.   Since her arrival to the Twin Cities, Karla has served as a dance specialist for Perpich Center for Arts Education’s ACE~Dance (Arts Courses for Educators) and Multicultural Voices programs and was an arts education coach for its ASAP (Arts & Schools As Partners) program.  She also served as a Teaching Artist and arts education coach for Arts for Academic Achievement (MPS).  She is a 2013 recipient of the Excellence in Educational Service Award (Ordway Center for Performing Arts).  She is currently a Teaching Artist and a design team member for Ordway’s Dance to Learn Program and is on several Teaching Artist rosters throughout the state of Minnesota, including COMPAS, The Givens Foundation for African American Literature, and MN State Arts Board. 

 


 

 

What or who inspired you to become a Teaching Artist?

 

Dance and creative writing are my main artistic media.  During my teen years, my first dance instructor and my high school AP creative writing teacher were my earliest influences.  The experiences with each of these professionals (in group settings and through one-on-one interaction) confirmed what I suspected about my artistic abilities.  I quickly developed a sense of purpose and identity and was empowered through my consistent engagement in the skill-building rigors of these disciplines.  My connection with these art forms and with those of like mind was grounding, energizing, and affirming, especially during some of the more tumultuous times of my youth.  I realized then that I would someday foster that same type of experience for others.

 

Not only did I possess a love of both the arts and education, but also the two always fit together so well in my thinking.  As a young adult, the arts quickly became a natural part of my pedagogical practice as an academic schoolteacher.  I used nonverbal communication exercises (movement, theater) to support English language learning in my classroom.  Later, I actively sought opportunities as a Teaching Artist as I became aware of the growing spectrum of available prospects.

 

What has been most helpful to you in your work?

 

Being part of a network of professionals (with a broad array of skills essential to the arts education professional) has been most helpful to my practice.  These connections have provided opportunities to exchange ideas, to collaborate, to support, and to challenge one another.

 

What have you learned are the unique talents of a Teaching Artist?

 

A Teaching Artist should:

  • Possess a high level of skill and knowledge (and a healthy dose of zeal) regarding one’s art form(s).

  • Have as much of a gift/passion/respect for teaching the art form as the art form itself.

  • Be dedicated to cultivating cooperative relationships with others at every possible level within partnering arts, educational, and civic institutions by:

1.   Learning, identifying, and sharing areas of common purpose among artists, educators, civil servants, and administrators.

2.   Identifying and utilizing common language that facilitates communication between partners of different sectors of the community during the planning, implementation, and assessment phases of a creative project.

  • Maintain a commitment to one’s own continued professional development.

  • Have a well-constructed plan and be able to make adjustments on the fly.

  • Have a respect for and understanding of the strengths and well as the challenges of the learners in the room.

What have you learned that a Teaching Artist needs to know?

 

A Teaching Artist has a duty as a lifelong learner and as such, never ‘arrives’.  Such understanding preserves a healthy sense of curiosity and integrity, which also informs the work with students.

 

What have you learned is the most important thing a Teaching Artists needs to be able to do?

 

We must ensure that the creative journeys we make with our students are just as satisfying as reaching our destinations.  Keeping our students fascinated and engrossed throughout each stage of the art making process fosters their ability to find fulfillment in the moment during the active pursuit of a goal.

 

What or who has been your best teacher?

 

My best teacher has always been my mother.  Not only does she have an impressive track record in youth development that spans several decades, but also her everyday life typifies the very values that she cultivates in young people.  One of the favorite lessons she taught me early in life is that active learning and fun need not be mutually exclusive experiences.

 

What is the most important advice you would give to a young artist about to enter the field?

 

Isolation can be a powerful foe in this business.  Build your own comprehensive support system as early as possible.  In addition to acquiring the necessary formal training, seek out arts advocacy organizations that can connect you to critical trade resources.  Whenever possible, attend industry-related workshops, panel discussions, forums, summits, and conferences, and be sure to engage and exchange information with others.  Avail yourself to print and electronic trade publications, including Teaching Artist handbooks, journals, newsletters, e-zines, etc.  Connect with others that have crossed hurdles that you are facing; there are many experienced Teaching Artists for whom mentorship is common practice.

 

Has there been one experience that immediately comes into your mind that changed the way you teach?

 

Some years ago, I was approached by an arts administrator who had witnessed my work with students in several different learning environments.  She remarked that I intuitively employed some very effective teaching strategies.  She also informed me that my ability to identify those strengths could be very beneficial to my further growth as a Teaching Artist.  I soon took part in several professional development opportunities, which opened a window to a different way of teaching – and learning.  As a result, I am better able to replicate effective learning experiences for my students.

 

What is your classroom experience encapsulates your thoughts on the work of a Teaching Artist?

 

Over the last two decades, I have taught in a wide variety of learning arenas and have worked with a diverse array of students (socio-economic, geographic, ethnic, etc.).  Fortunately, such experience has added more strategies to my pedagogical toolbox and has enabled me to sharpen and trust my instincts as to when and how to apply them. 

 

Additionally, growing up within an underserved demographic, I remained an ardent academic learner and achiever because the high expectations for learning asserted within my household always overrode the low ones that I often encountered in school and other areas of the community.  That same principle applies within the realm of arts learning.  Regardless of any other teaching strategies, lower expectations will always produce lower results.  It is for this reason that I bring those same high expectations for learning conveyed in my home into every classroom experience; my students respond in kind.

 

What sustained (s) your practice as a Teaching Artist?

 

Continuing to create and share new work with the public feeds my work as a Teaching Artist.  Setting new goals and challenging myself as an arts professional enhances my skill set, boosts my confidence, and refreshes my perspectives.  My professional artistic pursuits also inspire confidence in my students.

 

Moreover, my most difficult times as a Teaching Artist were those during which I experienced a sense of isolation.  This prevailed mostly in my early days in the field and during times of transition (e.g. working in a new city).  My practice has always thrived best when connected to the arts education community at large.
